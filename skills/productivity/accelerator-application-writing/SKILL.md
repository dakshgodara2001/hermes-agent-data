# Accelerator Application Writing

## When to use

Use this when writing or tightening answers for accelerator applications, grants, pitch forms, startup competitions, or founder profiles. Especially relevant for questions like:

- Relevant experience
- Startup description
- Tell us about the team
- Why now?
- Why this founder/team?
- What have you built?
- What problem are you solving?

## Core approach

If the application is about Arbiter / the agentic trading decision product, read `references/arbiter-application-positioning.md` before drafting.

1. **Answer the exact prompt first.** Do not write generic startup prose.
2. **Respect word limits aggressively.** If the user says 100 words, produce a final answer under 100 words and state the word count.
3. **Lead with the strongest proof.** For experience/team questions, include track record and wins, not just insight.
4. **Use concrete nouns.** Prefer shipped products, users served, systems built, roles, workflows, and evidence over adjectives.
5. **Avoid hype and legal risk.** Do not imply guaranteed returns, proven alpha, private data access, or regulated advice unless explicitly true and safe.
6. **Make the startup feel earned.** Connect the founder’s background to the problem through lived/professional exposure.
7. **Offer one best version first.** Optional variants can follow, but do not bury the recommended answer.

## Wins angle checklist

For “Relevant experience” and similar prompts, ensure the answer contains at least two of:

- Shipped 0→1 products
- Scaled or launched a measurable workflow
- Built systems for a named user group or count
- Reduced support/ops burden or improved a process
- Founder/startup experience
- Domain exposure that directly explains why this startup exists

If the copy only explains the founder’s insight but not their wins, it is incomplete.

## Team question pattern

If the founder is solo, be honest and make it sound intentional:

- “I’m currently the solo founder...”
- Explain why the founder has the right intersection for the wedge.
- Mention where feedback is coming from if real.
- Acknowledge missing depth without sounding weak: “I’m actively looking for market/compliance/brokerage-side advisors as the product matures.”

Do not invent advisors, cofounders, traction, or named experts.

## Startup description pattern

For “What problem are you solving? What are you building?” use this shape:

1. One-line product definition.
2. Specific problem with the current market/default behavior.
3. What the product does in workflow terms.
4. Why this is different from generic tools.
5. Concrete output or habit loop.

Example structure:

> [Product] is a [category] for [specific customer]. Today, [current solutions/default behavior] fail because [root cause]. We are building [workflow/system] that helps users [specific behavior change]. The first product [concrete output/habit]. Over time, it becomes [larger platform/workflow].

## Voice and style

- Direct, founder-like, specific.
- High-conviction but not inflated.
- Prefer active verbs: built, shipped, launched, reduced, scaled, observed, validated.
- Avoid startup clichés unless paired with concrete workflows.
- If the user challenges the angle, incorporate the correction immediately in the next draft.

## Pitfalls

- Do not over-index on “insight” and forget “wins” when the question asks for track record.
- Do not say “AI for trading” in a way that sounds like stock tips or easy money.
- Do not use long preambles when the user needs paste-ready application copy.
- Do not overclaim metrics or domain proof.
